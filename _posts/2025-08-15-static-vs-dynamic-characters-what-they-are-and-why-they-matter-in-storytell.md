Ever read a book and felt like the main character went on a life-changing journey while others seemed frozen in time? That’s the magic of *static vs dynamic characters*. In fiction, characters can either transform dramatically or stay pretty much the same from beginning to end. And both play an essential role in making a story work.

So, what is a dynamic character, and how do they compare to their less-evolving counterparts? Let’s dive into the difference between static and dynamic characters, look at examples, and explore why both are essential ingredients in the storytelling recipe.

## **What Is a Dynamic Character?**

A dynamic character is one who undergoes significant internal change over the course of a story. This could be a shift in personality, values, beliefs, or worldview. The transformation is usually prompted by the events of the plot or a series of revelations that challenge their original mindset.

-
  **Dynamic character definition:** A dynamic character is a literary figure who evolves meaningfully due to internal conflict, external pressure, or emotional experiences throughout the narrative.

In simpler terms, a dynamic character starts out one way and ends up another — ideally, for good reason.

## **Dynamic Character Example: Ebenezer Scrooge**

One of the most iconic dynamic character examples in literature? None other than Ebenezer Scrooge from *A Christmas Carol*. At the beginning, he’s cold, greedy, and downright unpleasant. But after a few ghostly visits, he emerges full of holiday cheer and newfound generosity. Classic redemption arc!

This shift isn’t just for show — it’s meaningful. Readers see Scrooge reflect, grow, and ultimately change who he is. That’s what makes him such a memorable, dynamic character.

## **What Is a Static Character?**

In contrast, a static character stays essentially the same throughout a story. [Their beliefs, behaviours, and personality](/writers-blog/how-to-write-believable-characters) remain consistent from start to finish. That’s not a bad thing — static characters often serve important roles, especially when supporting more change-focused leads.

-
  **A static character** doesn’t grow or evolve, and that stability can either be endearing, symbolic, or just plain entertaining.

## **Static or Dynamic Character? Why the Answer Matters**

When writers ask themselves whether a character should be a static or dynamic character, it often depends on the character’s role in the story. A well-crafted protagonist is often dynamic because they drive the plot with their internal struggles and growth.

But that doesn’t mean all great characters need to change. Just look at Sherlock Holmes — witty, eccentric, and unwavering from story to story. Fans *love* him because he *doesn’t* change. He’s a brilliant detective, full stop.

So in the battle of static vs dynamic characters, it’s not about which one is better. It’s about how they serve the story.

## **Protagonists Are Usually Dynamic (But Not Always)**

Most protagonists go on an emotional journey. Think of Hamlet wrestling with revenge, or Harry Potter discovering his purpose. These are rich**,** dynamic character examples because they learn, grow, and change through their experiences.

But static protagonists can work too, especially in genre fiction. Again, Sherlock Holmes fits the bill — he’s consistent, and we wouldn’t want him any other way.

## **Static Characters Make Great Foils**

Often, static characters exist to shine a light on the growth of others. These are called *foils* — characters who contrast with the protagonist to highlight their differences.

Take Dr. Watson, Sherlock’s loyal companion. He’s a static character through and through — humble, pragmatic, dependable. Next to Sherlock’s flamboyant brilliance, Watson’s steady nature helps emphasise Sherlock’s quirks and genius.

Foil characters like Watson don’t change, but they help us understand *how* the protagonist does (or doesn’t). In this way, static characters actually enhance a dynamic character’s arc.

## **Antagonists Are Often Static, Too**

Let’s be honest — most villains don’t do much soul-searching. That’s why many are static characters. Take Hannibal Lecter in *The Silence of the Lambs*. He’s terrifying from start to finish. But his role helps push Clarice Starling (the dynamic lead) to confront her past and evolve into a stronger, more confident agent.

Disney villains are also classic examples of static characters. They’re evil, they’re fabulous, and they stay that way. And we love them for it.

## **Static Characters Can Still Be Hilarious (or Infuriating)**

Sometimes, static characters are written purely for laughs or satire. Think Mr. Collins in *Pride and Prejudice*. He never learns, never changes — and that’s kind of the point. His ridiculousness contrasts perfectly with Elizabeth Bennet’s intelligence and independence.

In this case, the lack of change is a tool: it tells us something about the [world](/writers-blog/15-steps-to-fantasy-world-building) or the protagonist. Even the most absurd characters can serve a greater narrative purpose.

## **Dynamic Characters Drive the Plot**

Here’s the deal: dynamic characters don’t just *change* — they’re often *why* the story exists in the first place. Their inner struggles, flaws, and goals create the momentum that pushes the plot forward.

Sure, a static character might want to solve a mystery or find a treasure, but a dynamic character wants to *become* someone else in the process. They fail, grow, and learn as they chase that goal — and that’s where real storytelling lives.

In *The Great Gatsby*, Gatsby is a complex, dynamic character who chases an impossible dream. Tom Buchanan, by contrast, is a static character. Rich, arrogant, and unchanged, Tom’s presence in the story highlights Gatsby’s desperation and ultimate downfall.

## **Static and Dynamic Characters: The Perfect Pairing**

So, static vs dynamic characters isn’t a battle — it’s a partnership. Together, they [create tension](/writers-blog/creating-tension-when-writing-your-book), contrast, and clarity. Without one, the other might fall flat.

Dynamic characters create emotional stakes. Static characters provide structure and reflection. When used skillfully, they elevate each other and the entire story.

## **Final Thoughts**

Understanding whether a character is static or dynamic isn’t just useful for writing essays — it’s key to understanding how stories work.

A dynamic character changes in ways that mirror real human experience. A static character gives us stability, comedy, or a compelling foil. Both are vital in crafting a story that feels complete and compelling.

Next time you’re reading or writing, take a moment to ask: Who’s growing? Who’s not? And how does that make the story better?
