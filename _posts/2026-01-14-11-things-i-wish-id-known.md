You don't need genius to write a novel. You need persistence. That might sound like a platitude, but after going through the process of writing, editing, and self-publishing my first book, I can tell you it's the truest thing I know about writing.

Here are eleven things I wish someone had told me before I started.

## 1. Keep your idea simple

Your book will get complicated. You're going to be weaving together multiple plots and subplots, writing yourself into corners, and your characters are going to do unexpected things. So keep your original idea simple. The complexity will come whether you want it or not.

This is also important for when you try to sell your story. "A subtle satire of modern politics where a stranded astronaut accidentally causes a planet-wide rebellion" is a lot harder to pitch than "a fantasy heist derailed by romantic entanglements." You can add complexity, layers, and depth later. If you start with a full-scale GRRM universe, you're probably not getting to the end.

## 2. Know your characters deeply

A useful exercise I've found is to draft a one-page story for each main character. Where did they come from? What are the turning points in their lives? What made them the way they are? The more dramatic, the better.

## 3. Conflict in every scene

Conflict is the core of any good story. The hurdles your characters fail to overcome are what make them likeable. Conflict should be in every scene and every plotline. Your characters should hardly ever succeed — then when they finally do, it means something.

Conflict doesn't have to mean fights. Clashing motivations, unrequited love, a physical or mental barrier — these all count.

## 4. Your first draft is for you alone

That first draft is a mammoth effort. You'll work for months and end up with something almost like a book — structured, maybe even coherent — and completely unpublishable. And that's fine. Think of the first draft as you telling yourself the story.

Put it aside when you're done. Work on something else. Then come back and start again.

## 5. Editing is where the real work is

Getting to the end of a first draft means you've done more than 90% of writers ever do. But you're not done. Not by far.

Editing is an entirely different skill from writing. Many writers need to learn it almost from scratch. Be prepared for that.

## 6. Read voraciously in your genre

You can't write what you don't read. Not just to see what's popular, but to understand the conventions, the rhythm of the prose, the expectations readers bring to the genre.

## 7. Find beta readers you trust

Not your mum. Not your best friend who thinks everything you do is great. Find readers who'll tell you when something isn't working. They're worth their weight in gold.

## 8. The middle is the hardest part

The beginning is exciting. The end is in sight. But the middle — the long, grinding second act — is where most abandoned manuscripts live. Plan it. Know what happens there before you get there.

## 9. Don't talk about your book too much

There's a psychological phenomenon where talking about a goal gives you the same satisfaction as achieving it, which makes you less likely to actually do it. Write the book first, then talk about it.

## 10. Self-publishing is a real option

The traditional publishing route is long, competitive, and largely outside your control. Self-publishing has never been more accessible or legitimate. Do your research — both paths have real tradeoffs.

## 11. Finish it

Everything else is secondary to this. A finished imperfect book beats an unfinished perfect one every time. The only way to get better is to complete things and learn from them.

Whatever stage you're at — write the next word.
